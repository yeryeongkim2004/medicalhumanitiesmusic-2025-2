# Requiem for a dream

Darren Aronofsky’s Requiem for a Dream (2000) portrays the gradual destruction of four protagonists whose lives, bodies, and minds collapse under the weight of drug addiction, and music plays a central role in transforming this process from a medical condition into a lived, experiential narrative. The recurring soundtrack [“Lux Aeterna”](https://youtu.be/4I-GGTCV0K0?si=bfbtQzcWD52OO94N) functions not merely as background music but as an auditory representation of addiction’s compulsive and repetitive nature, aligning closely with what we discussed in Week 3 regarding the intersection of medical humanities and art. Rather than explaining addiction through clinical symptoms alone, the film uses music to foreground the subjective experience of loss of control, obsession, and psychological deterioration, thereby emphasizing illness as lived experience rather than disease as a biological fact. The rapid “hip-hop montage” sequences depicting drug use are tightly synchronized with the driving rhythm of the music, allowing sound to dictate the pace of visual editing and forcing the audience to physically feel the chaotic acceleration of addiction. The repetitive musical motifs mirror ritualized drug-taking behaviors, while the gradually intensifying tension reflects the narrowing of the characters’ inner worlds, in which desire replaces agency and the body becomes increasingly subordinated to compulsion. Through layered strings and relentless rhythmic patterns, the soundtrack conveys anxiety, fixation, and an inevitable downward spiral, enabling viewers to empathize with the protagonists’ psychological and bodily suffering in a way that purely visual storytelling could not achieve.

This musical representation of compulsive repetition and loss of voluntary control can be meaningfully compared to Maurice Ravel’s [Bolero](baek-jaehyuk.md), which we examined as an example of how music can embody psychological and neurological deterioration. Just as “Lux Aeterna” sonically structures addiction as an endless loop of craving and temporary relief, Bolero relies on an unchanging rhythmic pattern and a gradual crescendo to express obsessive persistence and escalating inner tension. In both works, repetition functions not as stability but as entrapment, emphasizing the erosion of autonomy that accompanies compulsive behavior. From the perspective of the Week 5 lecture on whether pain and suffering can be expressed through music, these works demonstrate that psychological distress does not need explicit narrative explanation to be understood; instead, sound itself becomes a medium through which inner suffering is externalized. In Requiem for a Dream, the audience does not simply observe addiction but experiences its oppressive rhythm bodily, while in Bolero, the listener is drawn into an inexorable buildup that mirrors the composer’s reported neurological decline and loss of control. In this way, music translates invisible mental suffering into a shared sensory experience, making pain and obsession perceptible and emotionally resonant.

Furthermore, the role of the body as both a medium and a limitation of artistic expression, discussed in Week 10 through the emergence of orthopedics and the study of musicians’ posture, provides an additional layer of interpretation. In Requiem for a Dream, the body is repeatedly shown as a site of breakdown, marked by exhaustion, deterioration, and ultimately irreversible damage, while the relentless musical rhythm underscores how physical endurance is pushed beyond its limits. Addiction strips the characters of bodily autonomy, reducing the body to an object governed by chemical dependency rather than conscious control, a theme that resonates with the historical medical concern for how bodies are disciplined, corrected, or damaged through repetitive action. Just as nineteenth-century orthopedics sought to optimize the musician’s body for sustained performance, Aronofsky’s film reveals the inverse process: the body collapsing under repetitive, destructive use. The contrast highlights how music can both depend on bodily control and expose its failure, reinforcing the idea that physical health, psychological stability, and artistic expression are inseparable.

Taken together, Requiem for a Dream and Bolero illustrate how music functions as a powerful bridge between medicine, psychology, and the humanities by transforming abstract concepts such as addiction, pain, and loss of control into tangible sensory experiences. By aligning musical repetition, rhythm, and tension with bodily and psychological decline, these works embody the core themes of the course: the importance of subjective illness narratives, the expressive capacity of art to convey suffering, and the central role of the human body in both experiencing and expressing distress. Music, in this sense, does not aestheticize suffering or soften its impact, but instead amplifies its reality, enabling audiences to encounter illness not as distant pathology, but as an embodied human experience.

다렌 아로노프스키 감독의 영화 *레퀴엠(Requiem for a Dream, 2000)*은 중독이 개인의 삶과 정체성, 그리고 신체와 정신의 통제력을 어떻게 붕괴시키는지를 집요하게 그려낸 작품이다. 이 영화에서 음악은 단순한 감정 유도 장치가 아니라, 중독이라는 질환의 심리적 구조와 반복적 강박을 드러내는 핵심적인 서사 장치로 기능한다. 클린트 만셀의 사운드트랙 [「Lux Aeterna」](https://youtu.be/4I-GGTCV0K0?si=bfbtQzcWD52OO94N)는 반복되는 리듬과 점진적으로 고조되는 긴장감을 통해, 중독이 지닌 집착적이고 순환적인 특성을 청각적으로 구현한다. 특히 약물 사용 장면에서 활용되는 이른바 ‘힙합 몽타주’ 기법과 결합될 때, 음악은 빠른 편집 리듬을 주도하며 관객으로 하여금 인물들이 경험하는 혼란과 과속 상태를 신체적으로 체감하게 만든다. 이러한 반복 구조는 중독 행위가 합리적 선택이 아닌, 통제력을 상실한 의례적 행동임을 강조하며, 중독의 끝없는 순환을 음악적으로 가시화한다 이러한 음악적 반복과 강박은 5주차 강의에서 다룬 ‘고통은 음악으로 표현될 수 있는가?’라는 질문과 직접적으로 연결된다. 레퀴엠에서 음악은 중독으로 인한 고통을 미화하거나 감정적으로 정리해 주지 않는다. 오히려 반복되는 리듬과 점점 증폭되는 사운드는 인물들이 빠져나올 수 없는 심리적 덫과 신체적 파괴 과정을 그대로 드러내며, 관객이 그 고통을 거리감 없이 경험하도록 만든다. 이는 고통이 음악을 통해 ‘아름답게 변환’되는 것이 아니라, 오히려 그 불편함과 압박감이 강화될 수 있음을 보여주는 사례라고 할 수 있다.

이와 유사한 방식으로 강박과 통제력 상실을 음악적으로 표현한 작품으로는 모리스 라벨의 [「볼레로(Boléro)」](baek-jaehyuk.md)를 들 수 있다. 「볼레로」는 처음부터 끝까지 거의 변화하지 않는 리듬과 선율이 지속되며, 점진적인 크레셴도를 통해 긴장감을 극도로 증폭시킨다. 이러한 구조는 자발적 통제가 점점 무너지고, 하나의 행동 패턴에 사로잡히는 상태를 상징적으로 보여준다. 레퀴엠에서 「Lux Aeterna」가 중독의 강박적 순환을 반영한다면, 「볼레로」는 반복과 집요함을 통해 신경학적 퇴행과 통제력 상실이라는 심리적·신체적 상태를 음악적으로 드러낸다. 두 작품 모두 음악 속 반복을 통해, 주체가 자신의 행동을 더 이상 통제하지 못하는 상태를 관객이 직접 ‘느끼게’ 만든다는 공통점을 지닌다. 이러한 분석은 3주차 강의에서 다룬 의료인문학과 서사의학의 관점과도 연결된다. 레퀴엠과 「볼레로」에서 음악은 질환을 객관적으로 설명하지 않는다. 대신 질환을 겪는 주체의 내면적 경험, 즉 불안, 집착, 상실감, 그리고 점진적인 붕괴를 비언어적으로 전달한다. 이는 질병(disease)을 진단명이나 증상으로 설명하는 것이 아니라, 질환경험(illness)을 중심에 두는 의료인문학적 접근과 맞닿아 있다. 관객은 음악을 통해 중독이나 신경학적 이상을 ‘이해한다기보다 체험’하게 되며, 이는 예술이 의학적 지식을 보완하는 방식 중 하나라고 볼 수 있다.

더 나아가 이러한 음악적 경험은 10주차 강의에서 다룬 ‘신체와 통제’의 문제와도 연관된다. 중독이나 강박은 단순히 정신의 문제에 그치지 않고, 신체의 리듬과 행동 패턴을 지배한다. 레퀴엠의 빠르고 반복적인 음악과 편집은 인물들의 몸이 더 이상 자율적으로 움직이지 못하고, 특정 자극에 반응하는 기계처럼 기능하게 되었음을 드러낸다. 마찬가지로 「볼레로」의 지속적인 리듬은 음악이 끝날 때까지 멈출 수 없는 신체적·정신적 강박 상태를 연상시킨다. 이는 인간의 몸이 예술적 표현의 도구이자, 동시에 질환이 각인되는 장소라는 점을 다시금 상기시킨다.

결국 레퀴엠과 「볼레로」는 음악이 심리적·행동적 장애를 단순히 묘사하는 수준을 넘어, 그 구조와 감각을 청각적으로 재현할 수 있음을 보여준다. 반복, 리듬, 점진적 긴장은 중독과 강박이라는 질환의 핵심 메커니즘을 드러내며, 관객으로 하여금 통제력 상실이라는 경험을 간접적으로 체화하게 만든다. 이러한 점에서 두 작품은 음악이 의료인문학적 맥락에서 고통과 질환을 이해하는 강력한 서사적 도구가 될 수 있음을 보여주는 중요한 사례라고 할 수 있다.

# The music I would like to be played at my own funeral

I would like [«River Flows in You»](https://youtu.be/7maJOI3QMu0?si=90CIItVzuiK4IQdc) by Yiruma to be played at my funeral, as it embodies a sense of calmness and warmth that I hope will be associated with my memory. The composition is structured around repeating piano patterns that flow gently, evoking the rhythm of quiet breathing. Its melody, though simple, is highly expressive, rising and falling in a manner that feels both natural and human. For me, this composition creates a moment of quiet reflection. It softens sadness and invites people to remember the tender moments rather than the painful ones. Its understated harmonies and gentle dynamics prevent emotional overwhelm, creating a soothing and contemplative atmosphere. This is the feeling I would wish to leave behind.

저는 제 장례식에서 이루마(Yiruma)의 [«River Flows in You»](https://youtu.be/7maJOI3QMu0?si=90CIItVzuiK4IQdc)가 연주되길 원합니다. 제 기억과 함께 사람들이 느끼길 바라는 차분함과 따뜻함을 담을 수 있을것 같아 이 곡을 선택하게 되었습니다. 반복되는 피아노 패턴이 부드럽게 흐르며, 마치 조용히 숨 쉬는 것처럼 편안한 느낌을 줍니다. 단순하지만 표현력이 풍부한 멜로디가 자연스럽게 오르내리며 인간적인 감정을 전합니다. 이 곡은 조용히 되돌아보게 하는 시간을 만들어 주며, 슬픔을 완화시키고 아픈 순간보다는 소중하고 따뜻한 기억을 떠올리게 합니다. 저는 사람들이 저를 기억할 때, 이 곡이 주는 그런 평온함과 따뜻함을 함께 느끼길 바랍니다.

# Explanation of the Relationship Between the HYQ Portfolio and the DB File: What Counts as Healing?

My QBL Future Question asks: when medicine, art, and technology all attempt to respond to human suffering, what can we legitimately call “healing”? This question emerged from a course that consistently challenged the assumption that healing equals elimination of pain. My engagement with music—through film analysis, historical case studies, and personal reflection—became a way to test this question across different modes of suffering and care. The three musical contexts I explored do not simply depict pain; each proposes a different relationship between suffering and healing. Together, they clarify why healing cannot be defined as a single outcome, but must instead be understood as a spectrum of responses to loss of control.

In «Requiem for a Dream», music does not function therapeutically. It does not soothe, guide, or resolve. Instead, it exposes the structure of addiction as an illness defined by repetition and compulsion. This directly aligns with the QBL distinction between disease and illness: while addiction can be medically described, its lived reality is cyclical, ritualized, and resistant to intervention. From the perspective of my QBL paper, this case demonstrates a critical boundary of healing. Medicine aims to interrupt pathology, yet here music reveals a condition in which interruption fails. The soundtrack does not reduce suffering—it forces recognition of its inescapability. This challenges a purely medical definition of healing and supports the QBL claim that not all suffering can be overcome through correction.

«Boléro» sharpens this challenge further. Unlike addiction, which still carries the illusion of choice, neurological degeneration removes agency altogether. The music’s structure embodies a condition where repetition is no longer expressive but compulsory. In QBL terms, this example destabilizes the idea that healing must involve improvement. If illness is progressive and irreversible, then medical success becomes ambiguous. Boléro suggests that in such cases, healing cannot mean restoration. Instead, meaning arises through recognition and accompaniment—a core idea in narrative medicine discussed throughout the course. This reinforces my QBL argument that care must adapt when cure is no longer possible. Music here does not fix deterioration; it gives form to an experience that medicine can diagnose but not transform.

My choice of «River Flows in You» for my funeral directly responds to this problem. Death represents the ultimate point at which medicine fails entirely. Yet the absence of cure does not eliminate the possibility of care. This is where my personal reflection becomes conceptually necessary, not optional. In QBL terms, this example illustrates healing as emotional containment rather than intervention. The music does not deny loss, but reshapes how it is shared. It creates a space in which grief can exist without escalation, aligning with the course’s emphasis on healing as relational and experiential. Here, music fulfills precisely the role my QBL paper assigns to art within the medical humanities: not as an alternative to medicine, but as a response to what medicine cannot resolve.

When placed in direct dialogue with my QBL paper, these three musical cases articulate a single argument: healing cannot be defined solely by the reduction of suffering. Sometimes, healing means exposure; sometimes, accompaniment; sometimes, quiet acceptance.
